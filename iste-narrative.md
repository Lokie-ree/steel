# ISTE 26 Narrative Brief

*Workstream 1 of the ISTE 26 research plan. The point of this document is field
calibration: getting the official framework language into the body, locking the
spine of the story, and identifying the three claims that don't need to be
spoken because the frameworks already say them.*

---

## The spine (the one-sentence claim everything supports)

**Geometry is built from operations students can feel — not formulas students
must accept. Creative Lab is a system of interactive R3F modules where the math
reveals itself through the student's own hands.**

Everything below is evidence for that claim, rewritten in the language
ISTE+ASCD has already committed to.

---

## Handshake sentences

Three sentences. Each one is the right thing to say in a specific conversational
moment — and the wrong thing to say in others. The "don't use when" field is
the discipline. A sentence overused stops being a handshake and becomes a
tagline.

### 1 — Peer-recognition handshake

> **"The interaction architecture is the pedagogy."**

- **Use when:** talking with a designer, developer, or technically-fluent
  educator who already understands that interactive ≠ decorative.
- **Why it works:** compresses the whole thesis into one move; the listener
  does the unpacking. The receipts are real — CSE, M1, M2, M3 all prove it.
- **Don't use when:** the listener doesn't have the word "architecture" in
  their working vocabulary. It'll sound like jargon, not insight.

### 2 — Framework-bridging handshake

> **"Pedagogy-first, carried into production."**

- **Use when:** the conversation already names a framework (UDL, TLPs, ISTE
  Educator Standards, Olga's Pedagogy-First Blueprint). Olga, Aleata, Monica.
- **Why it works:** uses the listener's framework vocabulary as the front
  half, and adds the thing the framework usually doesn't reach — actual
  shipped software — as the back half.
- **Don't use when:** the listener hasn't named a framework yet. The sentence
  has nothing to bridge from.

### 3 — Teacher-to-teacher handshake

> **"Geometry is built from operations students can feel — not formulas
> students must accept."**

- **Use when:** talking with a classroom teacher, a math coach, or anyone who
  lives in the felt experience of students disengaging from notation.
- **Why it works:** names the wound (formulas students must accept) before
  naming the alternative. The teacher has watched students *be that* kid.
- **Don't use when:** the listener is a researcher, administrator, or vendor.
  They'll hear it as pedagogy talk and tune to the structural conversation
  instead.

## Section A — How Creative Lab maps to the Transformational Learning Principles

The TLPs are the controlled vocabulary for ISTE+ASCD work. Every session at
ISTE 26 gets tagged into them. Eight sub-principles in three categories. Below,
the principle as ISTE defines it, then how it shows up in Creative Lab in
plain, specific language.

### Nurture

**Cultivate Belonging.** ISTE: relationship-building, safety, durable skills
like empathy and creativity. → In Creative Lab: the round-based architecture
is a *pacing contract* with the learner. Each round establishes what's safe to
try before the next one opens. No round-skipping, no high-stakes single
moments. The system says "you belong here, take your time."

**Connect Learning to the Learner.** ISTE: differentiation, learner
variability, advance toward mastery. → In Creative Lab: round-indexed copy in
`-copy.ts` files means the same module speaks differently at round 1 than at
round 4. Visibility flags via OR gates mean reveals are *earned*, not
broadcast — a student who's already grasped it doesn't get force-fed remedial
language.

**Ensure Opportunity.** ISTE: responsiveness to cultural and developmental
contexts, reducing barriers. → In Creative Lab: the modules require no prior
domain vocabulary to begin. The interaction is the entry point. A student who
doesn't yet have the word "dilation" can still drag the scale slider and *see*
the figure change. The math vocabulary attaches to the experience after the
experience.

### Guide

**Spark Curiosity.** ISTE: connect content to prior knowledge, create new
pathways for engagement. → In Creative Lab: this is the *home pillar*. The
Cross-Section Explorer's hexagon-from-cube cut is built specifically as a
"wait, WHAT?" moment. M3's earned reveal of the squares-on-sides geometry is
designed for the same beat. Curiosity is not decoration; it is the engineered
entry point of every module.

**Develop Expertise.** ISTE: framing learning around conceptual
understandings, multiple modes of presentation and inquiry, intentional
practice. → In Creative Lab: 8.G.A.1–8.G.B.8 is covered by three modules
sharing the same architectural patterns. The same student encounters rigid
motions, dilations, and Pythagorean theorem through the same interactional
vocabulary. Cross-module pattern consistency *is* a Develop Expertise claim
about teacher practice.

**Elevate Reflection.** ISTE: timely feedback, time and support to reflect.
→ In Creative Lab: the connection moment in the Cross-Section Explorer is a
reflective pause by design. After the student has completed both modes
(cutting and rotating) on the same solid, a sentence appears that names the
relationship between the two operations. It is a deliberate "now look back at
what you just did" beat.

### Empower

**Prioritize Authentic Experiences.** ISTE: real-world contexts, students as
designers, creators, problem-solvers. → In Creative Lab: students *operate
on* geometry rather than receive it. The joystick gizmo in CSE is not a
quiz interface; it is a manipulation interface. The student is the cutter,
not the cut.

**Ignite Agency.** ISTE: learner-led approaches, follow intellectual
pursuits, take risks, make discoveries. → In Creative Lab: the rotation mode
in CSE is a builder. The student decides which silhouette to revolve. M1's
reflection axis is dragged, not narrated. Discovery is the affordance, not the
goal.

**Bottom line for Section A:** Creative Lab lives primarily in **Guide**
(Spark Curiosity, Develop Expertise, Elevate Reflection) with strong showings
in **Empower** (Prioritize Authentic Experiences, Ignite Agency). Nurture is
present but secondary — the strongest Nurture claim is that the round-based
pacing system reduces fear of failure.

## Section B — Which Profile of an AI-Ready Graduate roles does this work cultivate?

Six roles per Culatta's ASCD blog (the canonical source): Learner, Researcher,
Synthesizer, Problem Solver, Connector, Storyteller. (Some secondary write-ups
call Problem Solver "Ideator." Use Culatta's word.)

Creative Lab is not an AI tool. It is, however, a system that builds the
*cognitive habits* the Profile names. Two roles in particular:

**Primary: Synthesizer.** Culatta: students bring information together from
various sources to increase understanding, illuminate connections, and
generate new solutions; they adapt information across formats and levels.
→ The Cross-Section Explorer's connection moment is literally synthesis: two
different operations (cutting a 3D solid; revolving a 2D silhouette) are
shown to produce structurally related objects on the same solid. That is
*the* Synthesizer move, performed in geometric form. Students are not just
seeing two activities — they are building the abstract structural identity
that links them.

**Strong secondary: Connector.** Culatta: facilitate deeper human
connections, identify shared values and goals, elicit diverse perspectives.
→ This one is less direct, but real: Creative Lab modules are designed to
be talked about. The reflection axis in M1, the scale factor in M2, the cut
plane in CSE are all *boardable* — a teacher can ask "what does the class
think happens if I do this?" and the manipulation gives the class a shared
referent. The module is a connector between students in a room.

**Honest non-fit: Learner, Researcher, Storyteller, Problem Solver as
Culatta defines them.** These four involve students *using AI tools* —
prompting AI for feedback, evaluating AI claims, brainstorming with AI,
generating media with AI. Creative Lab modules do none of that. Don't claim
this lane. It belongs to other presenters (Aleata Hubbard, Cristobal
Alvares, Monica Burns).

**Complementary axes, not opposed.** The AI-integration lane and the
embodied-geometry lane are *different axes* of humanizing math, not
competing claims to the same axis. Aleata Hubbard's CRSE work asks *whose*
identity and culture the tool reflects. Creative Lab asks *how* the math
becomes a thing the student operates on. A fully humanized math tool needs
both. Naming this distinction in conversation — especially with presenters
in the AI-integration lane — turns a forbidden-moves boundary into a
coherent position: I'm not claiming your lane; I'm working a complementary
one.

**The 30-second pitch line:**

> "Creative Lab modules build the Synthesizer habit before students ever
> touch an AI tool. The Cross-Section Explorer lets a student cut a cube and
> separately revolve a silhouette — and then realize, from their own hands,
> that those two operations are showing them the same underlying structure.
> That's synthesis as a felt experience. AI-readiness starts in the body."

## Section C — Three things that don't need to be said at ISTE because the frameworks already say them

These are the claims you no longer have to defend in conversation. The
official ISTE+ASCD frameworks defend them for you. Recognizing this is the
point of doing this research: it strips out the explanation overhead from
your in-person pitches.

**1. "Interactive matters more than informational."**
The TLPs make this claim institutionally. *Prioritize Authentic Experiences*
plus *Ignite Agency* together are an explicit institutional commitment to
learner-led interaction over content delivery. You don't have to argue for
interactivity — argue from it.

**2. "Curiosity is a serious instructional design target."**
*Spark Curiosity* is one of the eight TLPs, with formal definition,
self-assessment tools, and a research annotation document behind it. Culatta
wrote the headline blog. Scott Shigeoka was an ISTE 25 mainstage Featured
Voice on curiosity. The conference has already made curiosity a
first-class outcome. You can say "this is engineered for curiosity" and the
room will know what you mean.

**3. "Geometry should be felt before it is symbolized."**
This is the deeper bet. Two framework-level reasons not to argue it: (a) the
*Connect Learning to the Learner* principle establishes that meeting the
learner where they are is non-negotiable; (b) Culatta's framing of the
Profile of an AI-Ready Graduate is explicitly that it's "not a checklist of
tech skills, but a human-centered guide." The institutional posture is
already pro-embodied, pro-experiential. You don't have to defend "felt
before symbolized" from first principles — you can show it.

## Field calibration note (Task 3, condensed)

A scan of ISTE 25 sessions and the 26 program preview shows the prior-year
ecosystem is dominated by:

- AI integration and AI literacy (the single largest topic by session count)
- Instructional coaching and leadership
- Digital citizenship
- General edtech tools (Desmos, Canva, etc.)
- Immersive AR/VR as a *space* (the ISTEVerse / Innovation Arcade) more than
  as a *content delivery vehicle*

What is *not* densely populated: subject-matter-specific, content-deep,
production-grade interactive modules targeting named standards. Creative
Lab's lane — Mathematics + Innovative Learning Environments + 6-8 + WebGL/R3F
— is real, named in the topic taxonomy, but under-served. The presenter
research workstream should confirm this empirically, but the working
hypothesis is: **the room is not crowded in this lane.** Show up.
